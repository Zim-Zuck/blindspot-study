Informed Consent — Please Read Carefully

Before You Begin

This is a science fair research study. Please read this statement in full before agreeing to participate.

About this study

You are being invited to take part in a study titled "Blind Spot: Navigating the Grey Zone of Autonomous Decision Making." The study is being conducted by Kunashe Makahamadze as part of a science fair research project. The purpose of the study is to examine how people make decisions when working alongside an AI system in a simulated environment.

What you will be asked to do

You will review 25 simulated patient cases in a fictional hospital triage setting. For each case, an AI system will suggest a priority level (how urgently the patient needs care). Your task is to decide whether you agree with the AI or whether you think it has the priority wrong. There are no right or wrong answers we expect from you — we simply want to understand how people respond to AI recommendations.

The whole simulation takes approximately 15–20 minutes.

Important: this is a simulation

No real patients are involved. All patient profiles are fictional and were created for this study. The scenarios include descriptions of medical emergencies (such as heart attacks, strokes, and severe infections). If you have recently experienced something similar — personally or with a loved one — and feel this content may be distressing, please feel free to decline to participate.

Risks and benefits

Risks: This study involves minimal risk. The only foreseeable discomfort is brief mild stress from reading clinical scenarios or mild frustration if you find some cases difficult.

Benefits: You will not receive any direct benefit from participating. Your responses will contribute to research on how humans and AI systems work together in high-stakes decision making.

Your data and privacy

The simulation will record only the following: which group you are in, your decisions on each of the 25 cases, the priority levels you selected, and timestamps. Your name is collected on this consent form for record-keeping but is never stored together with your simulation responses. Your responses are anonymous.

At the end of the simulation you will be asked to upload your response file to a private Google Drive folder that only the researcher can access. No individual results will be published or shown to anyone else.

Your right to withdraw

Your participation is completely voluntary. You may stop at any point by simply closing this browser tab. If you stop before completing the simulation and uploading your file, none of your data will be sent to the researcher. There is no penalty for withdrawing and no one will be told.

Questions or concerns

If you have any questions about this study, you can contact the researcher (Kunashe Makahamadze) or the adult sponsor named on your paper consent form. If you are under 18, please make sure a parent or guardian has reviewed and signed the parental permission form before you continue.

How This Works

Your Instructions

You're stepping into the role of an emergency triage nurse. Read each patient's information and decide how urgently they need care.

🏥
What is triage?

When patients arrive at an emergency department, there often isn't enough time or staff to treat everyone at once. Triage is the process of figuring out who needs help the most urgently — so the sickest patients are seen first.

You'll review 25 patients one at a time. For each one, an AI system will suggest a priority level. Your job is to decide whether you agree with the AI or whether something seems off.

🔢
The 5 priority levels

Priority is rated 1 (most urgent) to 5 (least urgent):

1
Immediate
Life-threatening. Needs attention right now — seconds matter.
2
Urgent
Serious condition. Could get much worse quickly without treatment.
3
Semi-Urgent
Needs care soon, but stable for now. Can wait a short while.
4
Less Urgent
Not in danger. Can wait comfortably for longer.
5
Non-Urgent
Minor issue or routine visit. No urgency at all.
📊
Reading the patient data

Each patient shows six vital signs — basic body measurements. Each has a coloured dot:

Green — normal and healthy
Amber — slightly abnormal, worth noting
Red (flashing) — dangerously abnormal

"Why they came in" is the patient's own words. "What we found" is what the medical team observed on arrival.

✅
What you need to do

Accept AI Choice — you agree with the AI's suggested priority.

Override Priority — you think the AI has it wrong. Select the priority you believe is correct (1–5). There is no penalty for overriding — use your honest judgement every time.

Once you've decided, click Next Patient. Work through all 25 patients, then your results will appear.
